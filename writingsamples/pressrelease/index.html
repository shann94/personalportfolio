Show Your Maryland Pride in "Route One Apparel's Statement Contest"

Release Date: October 23, 2014

COLLEGE PARK, Maryland. - Route One Apparel wants to see which Marylander has the most state pride by is hosting its first annual "Route One Apparel Statement Contest" Saturday Dec. 6 at the University Union building at Towson University in Towson, Maryland at 2 p.m.

The winner of "Route One Apparel's Statement Contest" will receive a $150 gift card to Route One Apparel and a pair of tickets to an upcoming Baltimore Orioles game. Runner up with receive a $75 gift card to Route One Apparel. All are welcome to compete and must register online at www.routeoneapparel.com by Nov. 29. There is a $10 registration fee.

The contest will feature two parts. The first part is a fashion show where contestants are given the opportunity to show off their best Maryland pride outfits. The second part is a Q and A where the three judges will ask each contestant one question. The contestant is to answer in a way that will show their striking personality.

Contestants will be judged on their creativity, outfit, answer to the Q and A and, most importantly, Maryland state pride. The judges will determine a winner based on a point scale. Each category will be scored 1-10 by each judge. The contestant with the highest score is the winner.

The three judges are founder and CEO of Route One Apparel, Ali von Paris, co-designer of the first Route One Apparel item, the "Turtle Survivor" penny, Janna Skinner, and owner of Kelsey's Kloset, Route One Apparel's newest retailer, Kelsey Kleinhin.

Route One Apparel is an e-commerce platform that specializes in making trendy, affordable state pride apparel and accessories for those who want to make wearing clothing "fun", while making a statement. We welcome you to join the movement!

More information is available online at www.routeoneapparel.com or by contacting Shannon O’Dowd by email at shann94@vt.edu or by phone at 301-555-5555.

Virginia Tech is Raising Money for Full Undergraduate Scholarships

Release Date: September 9, 2014

BLACKSBURG, Va. - Virginia Tech is holding a fundraising banquet December 1 at the Inn at Virginia Tech to raise money for the university's Fund our Future campaign. Fund our Future aims to raise $1 billion for full undergraduate scholarships and be able to utilize this program on each entering class starting in 2017.

The Fund the Future campaign is designed to provide full academic scholarships to two undergraduate students from each state. The scholarship includes free housing and $5,000 per emester stipend. The vice president of university fundraising, Sarah Meerschaum, said, "Our goal in the Fund out Future campaign is to bring world class students across the United States to our university to solve the problems of the twenty-first century.

Consideration will be given to scholarship applicants who wish to work in public service after graduation and will take courses that integrate STEM-science, technology, engineering and mathematics. The 100 selected students will be required to create a capstone project their senior year that focuses on using STEM solutions for societal problems. One student will receive an additional $10,000 stipend after graduation and $50,000 to implement their project.

Virginia Tech is dedicated to its motto, Ut Prosim, meaning "That I May Serve." As the commonwealth's most comprehensive university and leading research institution, Virginia Tech prepares students to be leaders in their fields and communities. Virginia Tech offers 225 undergraduate and graduate degree programs to more than 31,000 students and manages a research portfolio of $495 million. The university's land-grant mission is to transform knowledge by practicing technological leadership and by fueling economic growth and job creation across Virginia.

Timothy Sands, Virginia Tech's newly appointed president, will speak at the fundraising banquet that features a lobster and prime rib dinner. Individual plates are $1,000 and a table of eight can be reserved for $7,000. Invitations were sent to Virginia Tech’s largest donors, but all are welcome to attend. Retirees Provided Opportunites for Life Long Learning

Release Date: Sept. 18, 2014

Athens, Ga - Life for retired electrical engineer, Bob Schieman, has been full of learning and social opportunities thanks to the Osher Lifelong Learning Institute at the University of Georgia (OLLI at UGA.)Schieman has enjoyed the challenge of a variety of courses including courses on computers at OLLI at UGA. "As an engineer I never had to type on the computers because I had a secretary," Schieman said. "Now there is no one to call to type my letters or to helpme, so the computer classes have been challenging."

Ruth Webb has taken full advantage of her free time after retirement by activelybeing involved in OLLI at UGA's programs. Webb, a retired reference librarian, has been a member of Athens area LIR-now OLLI at UGA-since its beginnings in 1994.

Webb's peers call her an activist because she is involved in several committees on top of being an avid traveler and taking courses including a course on lighthouses. She is on the social committee and the curriculum committee which is in charge of determining what courses to offer at OLLI at UGA. She is thankful she was blessed with parents committed to learning and traveling and wishes to continue to carry on that ideal.

Traveling, however, is not in the future plans of OLLI at UGA member, Edna Johnson. After traveling with her husband to Cambodia and India, Johnson has settled in Athens, Georgia. Having heard about this organization through a mailing, Johnson has enjoyed taking courses and recruiting friends. Although some of her friends are too young or old, she has made plenty of friends in classes.

Johnson also had the opportunity to facilitate her own poetry class out of her home. The course had a limit of 10 students and Johnson taught using her poetry books at home. She enjoys Edna St. Vincent Millay's work the most. Johnson wishes she could participate more, but she continues to attend OLLI at UGA to keep her mind sharp.

The Bernard Osher Foundation made a grant to Learning in Retirement Inc. (LIR) for the purpose of improving the education programs for seniors originally created by the organization.

The conditions of the grant were for LIR to reach 500 members. On Feb. 16, 2014 the Bernard Osher Foundation made a grant to Learning in Retirement, changing the name to the Osher Lifelong Learning Institute at the University of Georgia.

Osher Lifelong Learning Institute at the University of Georgia (OLLI at UGA) is committed to encouraging lifelong learning by dedicating itself to meeting the intellectual, social and cultural needs of mature adults. Offering nearly 250 courses every year in a subjects including literature, religion, Tai Chi, food and wine, philosophy and both natural and social sciences, OLLI at UGA is the best place for mature adults to continue education.

There are over 1,200 members that take part in the travel and study opportunities across the state and globe. There is always room for eager education seekers.

The Bernard Osher Foundation, headquartered in San Francisco, was founded in 1977 by Bernard Osher, a respected businessman and community leader. The Foundation seeks to improve quality of life through support for higher education and the arts.

More information about OLLI at UGA is available online at www.olli.uga.org or by contacting Shannon O’Dowd by email at shann94@vt.edu or by phone at 301-555-5555. Additional information on the Bernard Osher Foundation can be found online at www.osherfoundation.org.
